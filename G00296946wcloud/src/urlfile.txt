<p><b>Sir Charles Antony Richard Hoare</b> (<b>Tony Hoare</b> or <b>C. A. R. Hoare</b>) <span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks" style="font-size:85%; font-weight:normal;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">FREng</a></span><sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Fellows_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Fellows-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (born 11 January 1934)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> is a British <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> who has made foundational contributions to <a href="/wiki/Programming_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Programming languages">programming languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formal_verification" title="Formal verification">formal verification</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_computing" title="Concurrent computing">concurrent computing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His work earned him the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a>, usually regarded as the highest distinction in computer science, in 1980.
</p><p>Hoare developed the <a href="/wiki/Sorting_algorithm" title="Sorting algorithm">sorting algorithm</a> <a href="/wiki/Quicksort" title="Quicksort">quicksort</a> in 1959–1960.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He developed <a href="/wiki/Hoare_logic" title="Hoare logic">Hoare logic</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Axiomatic_semantics" title="Axiomatic semantics">axiomatic</a> basis for verifying <a href="/wiki/Correctness_(computer_science)" title="Correctness (computer science)">program correctness</a>. In the semantics of <a href="/wiki/Concurrency_(computer_science)" title="Concurrency (computer science)">concurrency</a>, he introduced the formal language <a href="/wiki/Communicating_sequential_processes" title="Communicating sequential processes">communicating sequential processes</a> (CSP) to specify the interactions of concurrent processes, and along with <a href="/wiki/Edsger_W._Dijkstra" title="Edsger W. Dijkstra">Edsger Dijkstra</a>, formulated the <a href="/wiki/Dining_philosophers_problem" title="Dining philosophers problem">dining philosophers problem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dblp_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-microsoft_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microsoft-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-monitors_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monitors-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He is also credited with development (and later criticism) of the <a href="/wiki/Null_pointer" title="Null pointer">null pointer</a>, having introduced it in the <a href="/wiki/ALGOL" title="ALGOL">ALGOL</a> family of languages. Since 1977, he has held positions at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_life">Education and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Hoare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tony Hoare was born in <a href="/wiki/Colombo" title="Colombo">Colombo</a>, Ceylon (now <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>) to British parents; his father was a colonial <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a> and his mother was the daughter of a tea planter. Hoare was educated in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Dragon_School" title="Dragon School">Dragon School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and the <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Canterbury" title="The King&#39;s School, Canterbury">King's School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bl-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He then studied <a href="/wiki/Literae_Humaniores" class="mw-redirect" title="Literae Humaniores">Classics and Philosophy</a> ("Greats") at <a href="/wiki/Merton_College,_Oxford" title="Merton College, Oxford">Merton College, Oxford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MCreg_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On graduating in 1956 he did 18 months <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_the_United_Kingdom#After_1945" title="Conscription in the United Kingdom">National Service</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MCreg_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> where he learned Russian.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoare_autobio_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoare_autobio-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He returned to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> in 1958 to study for a postgraduate certificate in <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MCreg_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and it was here that he began <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a>, having been taught <a href="/wiki/Autocode" title="Autocode">Autocode</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mercury" title="Ferranti Mercury">Ferranti Mercury</a> by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Fox" title="Leslie Fox">Leslie Fox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He then went to <a href="/wiki/Moscow_State_University" title="Moscow State University">Moscow State University</a> as a <a href="/wiki/British_Council" title="British Council">British Council</a> exchange student,<sup id="cite_ref-MCreg_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> where he studied <a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">machine translation</a> under <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andrey Kolmogorov</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoare_autobio_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoare_autobio-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_career">Research and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Hoare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1960, Hoare left the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and began working at <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Brothers_(computer_company)" title="Elliott Brothers (computer company)">Elliott Brothers Ltd</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MCreg_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> a small computer manufacturing firm located in London. There, he implemented the language <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_60" title="ALGOL 60">ALGOL 60</a> and began developing major <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoare81_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoare81-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was involved with developing <a href="/wiki/International_standard" title="International standard">international standards</a> in programming and informatics, as a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Information_Processing" title="International Federation for Information Processing">International Federation for Information Processing</a> (IFIP) <a href="/wiki/IFIP_Working_Group_2.1" title="IFIP Working Group 2.1">Working Group 2.1</a> on Algorithmic Languages and Calculi,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> which <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specified</a>, maintains, and supports the languages ALGOL 60 and <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He became the Professor of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">Computing Science</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_of_Belfast" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen&#39;s University of Belfast">Queen's University of Belfast</a> in 1968, and in 1977 returned to Oxford as the Professor of Computing to lead the <a href="/wiki/Programming_Research_Group" title="Programming Research Group">Programming Research Group</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Computing_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University Computing Laboratory">Oxford University Computing Laboratory</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Computer_Science,_University_of_Oxford" title="Department of Computer Science, University of Oxford">Department of Computer Science, University of Oxford</a>), following the death of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Strachey" title="Christopher Strachey">Christopher Strachey</a>. He is now an <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Emeritus Professor</a> there, and is also a principal researcher at <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hoare's most significant work has been in the following areas: his sorting and selection algorithm (<a href="/wiki/Quicksort" title="Quicksort">Quicksort</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quickselect" title="Quickselect">Quickselect</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hoare_logic" title="Hoare logic">Hoare logic</a>, the formal language <a href="/wiki/Communicating_sequential_processes" title="Communicating sequential processes">communicating sequential processes</a> (CSP) used to specify the interactions between <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Concurrent process">concurrent processes</a> (and implemented in various programming languages such as <a href="/wiki/Occam_(programming_language)" title="Occam (programming language)">occam</a>), structuring computer <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Monitor_(synchronization)" title="Monitor (synchronization)">monitor</a> concept, and the <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axiomatic</a> specification of <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apologies_and_retractions">Apologies and retractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Hoare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apologies and retractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speaking at a software conference in 2009, Tony Hoare apologized for inventing the <a href="/wiki/Null_pointer" title="Null pointer">null reference</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I call it my billion-dollar mistake. It was the invention of the null reference in 1965. At that time, I was designing the first comprehensive type system for references in an object oriented language (<a href="/wiki/ALGOL_W" title="ALGOL W">ALGOL W</a>). My goal was to ensure that all use of references should be absolutely safe, with checking performed automatically by the compiler. But I couldn't resist the temptation to put in a null reference, simply because it was so easy to implement. This has led to innumerable errors, vulnerabilities, and system crashes, which have probably caused a billion dollars of pain and damage in the last forty years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>For many years under his leadership, Hoare's Oxford department worked on formal specification languages such as <a href="/wiki/Communicating_sequential_processes" title="Communicating sequential processes">CSP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Z_notation" title="Z notation">Z</a>. These did not achieve the expected take-up by industry, and in 1995 Hoare was led to reflect upon the original assumptions:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Ten years ago, researchers into formal methods (and I was the most mistaken among them) predicted that the programming world would embrace with gratitude every assistance promised by formalisation to solve the problems of reliability that arise when programs get large and more safety-critical. Programs have now got very large and very critical – well beyond the scale which can be comfortably tackled by formal methods. There have been many problems and failures, but these have nearly always been attributable to inadequate analysis of requirements or inadequate management control. It has turned out that the world just does not suffer significantly from the kind of problem that our research was originally intended to solve.</p></blockquote>
